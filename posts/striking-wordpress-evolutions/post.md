🟦 2003 – Launch of WordPress 0.7
🔹 Created by Matt Mullenweg and Mike Little, it was born as a fork of b2/cafelog. 
Impact: Brought a new vision to the blog: free, open source and with an active community. 

⸻
🟦 2004 – Introduction of plugins
🔹 WordPress 1.2 introduced plugin support. 
Impact: Opened the doors to infinite customization, giving rise to one of the largest ecosystems of web extensions. 

⸻
🟦 2005 – Default theme and static pages
🔹 WordPress 1.5 brought the “Kubrick” theme and support for pages, not just posts. 
Impact: WordPress stopped being just a blog and started becoming a true CMS. 

⸻
🟦 2008 – New administrative panel
🔹 WordPress 2.5 completely redesigned the dashboard. 
Impact: Dramatically improved usability and paved the way for adoption by non-technical users. 

⸻
🟦 2010 – WordPress Foundation
🔹 Creation of the non-profit foundation to guarantee the future of WordPress. 
Impact: Established commitment to free software and open governance. 

⸻
🟦 2013 – MP6: The modern redesign of the panel
🔹 Project MP6 changed the look of the admin with a cleaner, more responsive and modern interface. 
Impact: Standardized the user experience and set the stage for the next phase of WordPress. 

⸻
🟦 2016 – REST API integrated into core
🔹 WordPress 4.7 made the REST API officially part of the CMS core. 
Impact: Made WordPress a powerful headless tool, expanding its integration with apps, SPAs and modern systems. 

⸻
🟦 2018 – Gutenberg (Block Editor)
🔹 WordPress 5.0 released the block editor as default. 
Impact: Revolutionized content creation with a modular, visual approach. 

⸻
🟦 2021 – Full Site Edition (FSE)
🔹 Start of support for full site editing with blocks. 
Impact: Users can now customize headers, footers and templates without programming. 

⸻
🟦 2024 – Performance and AI
🔹 Increasing focus on performance, interoperability with APIs and experimental use of generative AI. 
Impact: WordPress remains competitive with modern stacks, integrating cutting-edge features with CMS familiarity.
